The Story

How I Got Here

I started at Verisk — one of the largest insurance data and analytics companies in the world — writing pipelines for workloads that processed over a million records a day. No ramp-up period, no toy datasets. Production from day one. That environment shaped everything about how I think: you build it right, or you get paged at 2am. There is no middle ground.

While working full-time, I went back to finish my Computer Science degree at SUNY Old Westbury — and ended up as a Teaching Assistant for graduate-level data engineering students. Helping 40+ engineers understand distributed systems, debug their pipelines, and think like production engineers forced me to articulate things I'd absorbed without examining. Why idempotency matters. What makes a DAG brittle. How to actually read an execution plan. Teaching made me a better engineer. It confirmed something I suspected: if you can't explain it simply, you don't fully understand it yet.

At KWI, I had the kind of opportunity that doesn't come often — build a production data platform from scratch, end to end, without inheriting someone else's debt. I designed the medallion architecture, the orchestration layer, the data quality framework, and then went further: I architected the company's first AI-powered semantic search system using RAG, pgvector, and Google Gemini embeddings. That system delivers sub-second product intelligence across 1,700+ records and eliminated hours of weekly manual work. I'm proud of it — not because it looks impressive on paper, but because it genuinely changed how a team operates every single day.

Defining Moments

Three Things That Shaped My Engineering

  1. 01

    Verisk Analytics · 2019

    Owning enterprise-scale pipelines from day one

    My first production role put me in front of insurance pipelines handling 1M+ records daily on Amazon Redshift. No handholding, no onboarding grace period. I learned fast that in production, the cost of a bad assumption isn't a failed test — it's corrupted data quietly reaching three downstream teams before anyone notices. That experience gave me a permanent instinct for defensive design: idempotency, schema contracts, and failure alerting built in before anything touches production.

  2. 02

    SUNY Old Westbury · 2023–2024

    Teaching graduate engineers while being one

    Serving as a TA while working as an active data engineer meant translating production reality into something 40+ students could act on immediately. That dual role forced a kind of clarity I hadn't needed before — you can't lean on jargon when a student is staring at you waiting for the actual explanation. The best engineers I've worked with all share one quality: they make complexity disappear for others. I decided that was a standard worth holding myself to.

  3. 03

    KWI · 2024–Present

    Building KWI's first AI system — and making it stick

    Anyone can stand up a prototype. Getting a RAG pipeline into production — with monitoring, embedding refresh logic, failure recovery, and feedback classification running on live data — is a fundamentally different problem. I designed and shipped it end-to-end: offline indexing pipeline, online query path, observability layer. It's not a demo. It runs in production daily, business decisions are made from it, and it was designed to be maintainable by whoever inherits it next.

Philosophy

How I Think About Systems

My default is boring by design. Not because I lack ambition — but because I've seen what happens when clever architectures meet the reality of on-call rotations, engineers who didn't write it, and hard deadlines. The systems that hold up are the ones that are straightforward to debug, easy to hand off, and completely unsurprising to operate.

My benchmark for a good data system is simple: does it run without surprises? When it fails, does the right person find out before a downstream user does? Could an engineer who has never touched it understand it six months from now? If the answer to any of those is no, the system isn't finished.

  • Determinism over cleverness: Idempotent pipelines, safe backfills, reproducible outputs. Reruns should never become incidents.
  • Visibility as a feature: SLA monitoring, lineage signals, and alerts that actually mean something. Not noise, not silence.
  • Contracts protect teams: Schema agreements, versioned migrations, staged rollouts. Downstream consumers shouldn't be afraid of your deploys.
  • Complexity is a cost: Every architectural decision should justify its weight. The right answer is usually the simpler one.

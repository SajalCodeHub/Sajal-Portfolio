Engineering Playbook

How I Think About Every System

Not principles I've read. Things I've learned from watching things break.

Idempotency first
Every pipeline should be safe to rerun on the same partition without producing duplicates or overwriting good data. Backfills will happen. Design for them from the start, not after the first incident forces you to.
The right person finds out first
SLA monitoring, lineage signals, and alerts that actually mean something — not a flood of noise that engineers start ignoring after two weeks. The goal is: on-call engineer knows before any downstream user does.
Quality gates, not quality reports
A check that produces a report nobody reads isn't a data quality system — it's false confidence. Checks that block bad data from moving forward are the only kind worth building. Everything else is theater.
Contracts protect teams
Schema agreements, versioned migrations, and staged rollouts let downstream consumers depend on your data without holding their breath every deployment. The handshake matters as much as the data itself.
Tune against evidence
Partition strategies, broadcast join decisions, index redesigns — all of these should come from execution plan analysis and before/after benchmarks, not from what intuition says should help.
Boring systems run longer
The cleverest solution is rarely the right one when the next person to touch it is an on-call engineer at 2am who didn't write it. Simple, well-understood patterns beat elegant complexity almost every time in production.
